Technology

The engineering behind on-device vision.

A closer look at the hardware, the models, the data that moves between them, and the questions that come up most often when evaluating SensorDyme for a pilot.

Architecture

Edge-first, by design.

Most computer vision products are built cloud-first and adapted for the edge afterward. SensorDyme is built the other way around.

Hardware. SensorDyme runs on Raspberry Pi 5 class hardware — a widely available, low-cost, low-power single-board computer. There is no GPU, no external accelerator, and no dependency on specialized silicon.

Models. Perception is split across two model families chosen for their efficiency on CPU: MediaPipe handles facial landmarks and full-body pose, while YOLO-nano handles lightweight object detection where it's needed. Both are small enough to run continuously without thermal throttling.

Runtime. Everything — capture, inference, and event generation — executes on the device in a single local pipeline. There is no round trip to a server in the critical path, so the system keeps working through network outages and continues to meet its frame-rate target regardless of connection quality.

Throughput. In typical deployments, the platform sustains 25–30 frames per second per camera, which is enough headroom for real-time attention, presence, and pose workloads without dropping frames under normal load.

Privacy

What actually leaves the device.

The short answer: numbers, not images. Here's what that means in practice.

On-device processing

Frames are captured, run through inference, and discarded in the same pipeline cycle. No frame is ever written to disk or queued for transmission.

Landmarks, not video

Model outputs are coordinates, scores, and counts — a pose skeleton, an attention score, an occupancy number. None of these can be reversed into an image.

No cloud dependency

The device functions fully offline. When connectivity is available, only the resulting events are sent onward — never the source frames.

FAQ

Common questions

How accurate is the detection?

Accuracy varies by module and installation conditions such as camera angle, lighting, and mounting distance. In pilot deployments we validate accuracy against ground-truth footage for each specific site before a system is relied upon operationally, and we report those figures directly rather than a single blanket number.

What does installation involve?

Each unit is a compact device with a camera module, mounted with a bracket at the appropriate height and angle for its task. It needs power and, optionally, a network connection for reporting. Most single-camera installations take under an hour.

How is data handled?

Video frames are processed on-device and never stored or transmitted. Only derived events — scores, counts, and alerts — leave the device, and only when connectivity is configured. There is no central video archive to manage, secure, or delete.

What's the pricing model?

Pricing combines a one-time hardware cost per device (under $150) with a software license per active module. There is no charge for data storage or bandwidth, since no video data is transmitted or stored.

What does the pilot process look like?

Pilots typically start with a short scoping call, followed by a fixed-term deployment of one to a handful of devices at a single site. We review accuracy and operational fit together at the end of the term before discussing a broader rollout.

Can exam proctoring run without recording students?

Yes — the proctoring module outputs gaze and attention signals only. No frame is ever written to disk, so there is no recording to store, request, or accidentally retain past the exam window.

How does SensorDyme integrate with our existing systems?

Each module emits structured events — a score, a count, an alert — over a simple local API. Most sites route these into whatever ticketing, dashboard, or notification tool they already use rather than adopting a new one.
